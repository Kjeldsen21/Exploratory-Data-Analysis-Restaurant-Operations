# Findings & Insights


### Dataset Overview
- **12,234** order lines with **5,370** unique orders
- **32 total menu items** across 4 categories: American, Asian, Mexican, Italian
- **Time period:** 3 Months (January 1 – March 31, 2023)


### 1. Revenue & Category Performance 
| Category | Revenue | Order Volume |
|---|---|---|
| Italian | $49,462.70 | 2,948 (2nd) |
| Asian | $46,720.65 | 3470 (1st) |
| Mexican | $34,796.80 | 2945 (3rd) |
| American | $28,237.75 | 2734 (4th) |
 
**Total Revenue: $159,217.90**

The Italian category generates the most revenue despite not being the most ordered category.
Asian cuisine leads on volume but falls second on revenue which results from Italian's higher average price point.
American sits last on both metrics despite being the most accessible price point on the menu.
 
**Insight:** This is a prime example of volume vs. margin split. Italian items generate
more value per order while Asian drives traffic. A restaurant owner should
consider the following options:
- Push Italian dishes more aggressively, whether by expanding the menu, offering Italian specials, etc.
- Leverage the Asian dishes popularity for upselling opportunities.


### 2. Menu Performance

Items priced above the menu average ($13.29) are predominantly Italian and Asian which are also the two highest revenue and volume generating categories.
This implies that premium pricing in these categories is not deterring customers. Vice versa, the more accessible pricepoint for the Mexican and American categories
do not generate enough interest to drive revenue and volume.
 
**High volume, high price (the sweet spot):**
Items like Korean Beef Bowl ($17.95) and Pork Ramen ($17.95) appear in the
above-average order frequency list despite costing above the menu average ($13.29), indicating strong customer demand and willingness to pay for Asian cuisine specifically.
 
**Potential dead weight:**
The bottom 10 least ordered items are worth reviewing. Items with low order
volume and low price (e.g. Edamame at $5.00) add cost and kitchen complexity without meaningful revenue contribution.

 
### 3. Order Volume Trends
 
- Orders were consistent across the 3-month period with no significant drop-off
- Average order size of 2.28 items suggests most customers order a main plus one additional item, presenting an opportunity for upselling
- Peak hours and busiest days identified through time-based analysis provide actionable staffing, inventory management and prep insights

**Insight from food service experience:** A 2.28 average items per order is relatively low. In a restaurant environment, this implies that non-main dishes (appetizers, sides, desserts)
are being under-ordered either due to server recommendations or menu design/placement. Targeted upselling on sides and appetizers presents an opportunity to increase average order volume.

 
### 4. High Value Orders
 
Analyzing the datasets shows that high-ticket orders consists of 4+ items, suggesting group dining drives disproportionate revenue. This is a common pattern in every dining environments.
 
**Insight:** Growing the group dining segment (larger orders, more items per visit) should be a commercial priority. Promotions targeting
group orders (Tiered Pricing, BOGO's, Family/Group Combos, etc.) or family-style meals could increase average order value across the dataset.


### 5. Limitations
 
- Dataset does not include party/table size, making it impossible to distinguish solo diners from group orders beyond item count
- 3-month period is too short to draw insights, patterns and strategies from
- Fictional Dataset: The findings and techniques applied are contextually useful but are not from a live restaurant environment
